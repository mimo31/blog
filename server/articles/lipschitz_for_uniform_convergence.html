It was once during an analysis lecture that my mind wandered off... We were discussing how uniform convergence was stronger than pointwise convergence. However, from the examples we saw, it seemed to me that for a sequence to converge pointwise but not uniformly, it had to have something special about it. So the question is: Just how much do we have to restrict a sequence of functions in order for pointwise convergence to also imply uniform convergence?

So a little problem was born. First, a definition:

Definition (\(\lambda\)-Lipschitz)
Let \(\lambda\in\rr,\lambda>0\) and \(f\) be a function \(f:D\to\rr\) for some \(D\subseteq \rr\). We say that \(f\) is \(\lambda\)-Lipschitz if and only if $$ \forall x_1,x_2\in D:\;\;\lvert f(x_1)-f(x_2)\rvert\leq\lambda\lvert x_1-x_2\rvert. $$ (see also Wikipedia)

With that, I can present to you my problem:

The Problem
Let \(\lambda\in\rr,\lambda>0,D\subseteq\rr\) with \(D\) bounded and \((f_n)_{n\geq1}\) be a sequence of \(\lambda\)-Lipschitz functions \(f_n:D\to\rr\), which converges pointwise to some \(f:D\to\rr\).

PROVE OR DISPROVE:
\((f_n)_{n\geq1}\) also converges to \(f\) uniformly.
